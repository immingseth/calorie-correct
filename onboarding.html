Design notes

Five screens, not seven. Most apps drag onboarding to ten or twelve screens to "qualify" the user — diet style, workout frequency, dietary restrictions, motivation level, etc. We're skipping all of it. Anything we don't strictly need to start the math gets asked later, in context, when it matters. The full math needs sex/age/height too — I bundled that into a hidden step right after Welcome (not shown here because it's visually a boring form). Could also collect it after first weigh-in if we want to keep the funnel even tighter.

The "weeks not days" screen is doing the most work. It's where we set the brand promise out loud: this is going to be honest, slow, and steady. Anyone looking for a 30-day quick fix should bounce here, and that's fine — they wouldn't have stuck around anyway. The closing italic line — "the scale will lie to you daily and tell the truth weekly" — is the kind of thing that gets screenshot and shared.

Scale step is positioned as table-stakes, not optional. We're a scale-first product. The third option ("I don't have a scale yet") nudges toward buying one — eventually we could affiliate-link a Renpho or Withings here, which is a small revenue line and aligned with the user's interest.

The "Done" summary previews the system. Seeing "First calibration: Sunday, May 18" right at the end of onboarding tells the user this isn't just another logger — there's a system that's going to do something for them. Anchoring the first delight moment.

What's not here. Permissions screen (notifications, HealthKit), email/account creation, and the paywall reveal. All of those would slot in around or after these five. The paywall in particular is worth its own design pass — I'd put it after the user logs their first meal, not before, so they feel value before being asked to pay.
